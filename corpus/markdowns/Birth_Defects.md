# Birth Defects

<h3>What are birth defects?</h3>
<p>A birth defect is a problem that happens while a <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/fetalhealthanddevelopment.html">baby is developing</a> in the mother's body.  Most birth defects happen during the first 3 months of pregnancy. One out of every 33 babies in the United States is born with a birth defect.</p>

<p>A birth defect may affect how the body looks, works, or both. Some birth defects like <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/cleftlipandpalate.html">cleft lip</a> or <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/neuraltubedefects.html">neural tube defects</a> are structural problems that can be easy to see. Others, like <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/heartdiseases.html">heart disease</a>, are found using special tests. Birth defects can range from mild to severe. How a birth defect affects a child's life depends mostly on which organ or body part is involved and how severe the defect is.</p>

<h3>What causes birth defects?</h3>
<p>For some birth defects, researchers know the cause. But for many birth defects, the exact cause is unknown. Researchers think that most birth defects are caused by a complex mix of factors, which can include:</p><ul>
<li><strong>Genetics.</strong> One or more genes might have a change or mutation that prevents them from working properly. For example, this happens in <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/fragilexsyndrome.html">Fragile X syndrome</a>. With some defects, a gene or part of the gene might be missing.</li> 
<li><strong>Chromosomal problems.</strong> In some cases, a chromosome or part of a chromosome might be missing. This is what happens in <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/turnersyndrome.html">Turner syndrome</a>. In other cases, such as with <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/downsyndrome.html">Down syndrome</a>, the child has an extra chromosome. </li> 
<li><strong>Exposures to <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/pregnancyandmedicines.html">medicines</a>, chemicals, or <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/reproductivehazards.html">other toxic substances</a>.</strong> For example, alcohol misuse can cause <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/fetalalcoholspectrumdisorders.html">fetal alcohol spectrum disorders</a>.</li>  
<li><strong><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/infectionsandpregnancy.html">Infections during pregnancy</a>.</strong> For example, infection with <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/zikavirus.html">Zika virus</a> during pregnancy can cause a serious <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/brainmalformations.html">defect in the brain</a>.</li> 
<li><strong>Lack of certain nutrients.</strong> Not getting enough <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/folicacid.html">folic acid</a> before and during pregnancy is a key factor in causing neural tube defects.</li> 
</ul>

<h3>Who is at risk of having a baby with birth defects?</h3>
<p>Certain factors may increase the chances of having a baby with a birth defect, such as:</p><ul>
<li>Smoking, drinking alcohol, or taking certain "street" drugs <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/pregnancyandsubstanceuse.html">during pregnancy</a></li>
<li>Having certain medical conditions, such as <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/obesity.html">obesity</a> or uncontrolled <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/diabetes.html">diabetes</a>, before and during pregnancy</li>
<li>Taking certain medicines</li>
<li>Having someone in your family with a birth defect. To learn more about your risk of having a baby with a birth defect, you can talk with a <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/geneticcounseling.html">genetic counselor</a>,</li>
<li>Being an older mother, typically over the age of 34 years</li>
</ul>

<h3>How are birth defects diagnosed?</h3>
<p>Health care providers can diagnose some birth defects during pregnancy, using <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/prenataltesting.html">prenatal testing</a>.  That's why it important to get regular <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/prenatalcare.html">prenatal care</a>.</p> 

<p>Other birth defects may not be found until after the baby is born. Providers may find them through <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/newbornscreening.html">newborn screening</a>. Some defects, such as club foot, are obvious right away. Other times, the health care provider may not discover a defect until later in life, when the child has symptoms.</p> 

<h3>What are the treatments for birth defects?</h3>
<p>Children with birth defects often need special care and treatments. Because the symptoms and problems caused by birth defects vary, the treatments also vary. Possible treatments may include surgery, medicines, <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/assistivedevices.html">assistive devices</a>, physical therapy, and speech therapy.</p> 

<p>Often, children with birth defects need a variety of services and may need to see several specialists. The primary health care provider can coordinate the special care that the child needs.</p>

<h3>Can birth defects be prevented?</h3>
<p>Not all birth defects can be prevented. But there are things you can do before and during pregnancy to increase your chance of having a healthy baby:</p><ul>
<li>Start prenatal care as soon as you think you might be pregnant, and see your health care provider regularly during pregnancy</li>
<li>Get 400 micrograms (mcg) of folic acid every day. If possible, you should start taking it at least one month before you get pregnant.</li>
<li>Don't drink alcohol, smoke, or use "street" drugs</li>
<li>Talk to your health care provider about any medicines you are taking or thinking about taking. This includes prescription and over-the-counter medicines, as well as dietary or herbal supplements.</li> 
<li>Learn how to prevent infections during pregnancy</li>
<li>If you have any medical conditions, try to get them under control before you get pregnant</li>
</ul>
