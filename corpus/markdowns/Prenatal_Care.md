# Prenatal Care

<h3>What is prenatal care?</h3>
<p>Prenatal care is the health care you get while you are <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/pregnancy.html">pregnant</a>. It includes your checkups and <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/prenataltesting.html">prenatal testing</a>. Typically, a health care provider specializing in the delivery of babies and the care of pregnant women, such as an obstetrician or a midwife, provides prenatal care.</p> 

<h3>Why do I need prenatal care?</h3>
<p>Prenatal care can help keep you and your fetus healthy until you're ready <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/childbirth.html">to deliver your baby</a>. It lets your provider spot health problems early. Early treatment can cure many problems and prevent others from developing.</p> 

<p>Every prenatal visit offers you the chance to ask questions about having a healthy pregnancy and preparing for delivery. Talk to your provider about childbirth education classes for you and your partner to help you prepare for the birth of your baby.</p>

<h3>How often do I need prenatal visits?</h3>
<p>Your provider will give you a schedule for your prenatal visits. Usually, visits during your pregnancy occur:</p>
<ul>
<li>Once a month for weeks 4 through 28</li> 
<li>Twice a month for weeks 28 through 36</li> 
<li>Weekly for week 36 until your baby is born</li>
</ul>

<p>If you are over 35 years old or your pregnancy is high risk because of <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/healthproblemsinpregnancy.html">health problems</a> like <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/diabetesandpregnancy.html">diabetes</a> or <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/highbloodpressureinpregnancy.html">high blood pressure</a>, your provider will probably want to see you more often. You can also expect to see your provider more often as your due date gets closer.</p> 

<h3>What happens during prenatal visits?</h3>
<p>During prenatal visits, your provider will check your health and carefully monitor your pregnancy until you deliver your baby.</p> 

<p><strong>Your first prenatal visit may take a little longer. Your provider will most likely:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Ask about your health history, including prior pregnancies</li>
<li>Ask about your family's health history</li>
<li>Discuss any health conditions and risk factors that could affect you or your fetus</li>
<li>Do a complete physical exam, including a pelvic exam and <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/pap-smear/">Pap test</a></li>
<li>Review blood, urine, or other routine prenatal lab tests</li>
<li>Check your blood pressure, height, and weight</li>
<li>Discuss a healthy amount of weight gain, <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/pregnancyandnutrition.html">healthy eating</a>, and prenatal vitamins</li>
<li>Figure out your due date (when you are 40 weeks pregnant)</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>During most visits, your provider will most likely:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Check your blood pressure and weight gain</li>
<li>Measure your abdomen (belly) to check <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/fetalhealthanddevelopment.html">fetal growth</a> once you begin to show</li>
<li>Check the fetal heart rate</li>
<li>Check your hands and feet for swelling</li>
<li>Do routine prenatal tests</li>
</ul>

<p>Depending on the stage of your pregnancy, you may have additional tests.</p>

<p>Always talk to your provider before you <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/pregnancyandmedicines.html">start or stop any medicine</a>. Some medicines can harm your fetus. To help keep yourself and your fetus healthy, <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/pregnancyandsubstanceuse.html">avoid substances</a> that could be harmful, such as tobacco, alcohol, and drugs.</p>
