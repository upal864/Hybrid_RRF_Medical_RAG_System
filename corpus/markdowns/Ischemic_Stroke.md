# Ischemic Stroke

<h3>What is an ischemic stroke?</h3>
<p>A <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/stroke.html">stroke</a> happens when there is a loss of blood flow to part of the brain. Your brain cells cannot get the oxygen and nutrients they need from blood, and they start to die within a few minutes. This can cause lasting brain damage, long-term disability, or even death.</p>

<p>There are two main types of strokes, ischemic and <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/hemorrhagicstroke.html">hemorrhagic</a>. Ischemic stroke is the more common type. An ischemic stroke happens when a <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/bloodclots.html">blood clot</a> blocks a blood vessel in the brain. Quick treatment can save a life and improve the chances of recovery.</p>  

<h3>What causes an ischemic stroke?</h3>
<p>Ischemic strokes are usually caused by one or more of the following:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>A blood clot.</strong> A clot can form in an artery that supplies the brain (thrombosis), or it can travel from another part of the body (embolism).</li>
<li><strong><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/atherosclerosis.html">Atherosclerosis</a>.</strong> A disease in which plaque, a sticky substance made up of cholesterol, fat, and other substances, builds up inside your arteries.</li>
<li><strong>Irregular heart rhythms.</strong> Conditions like <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/atrialfibrillation.html">atrial fibrillation</a> can cause clots to form in the heart, which may travel to the brain.</li>
<li><strong>Other heart and blood conditions.</strong> Such as <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/heartvalvediseases.html">heart valve diseases</a> or <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/sicklecelldisease.html">sickle cell disease</a>.</li>
</ul>

<p>A <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/transientischemicattack.html">transient ischemic attack</a> (TIA) is caused by a brief blockage of blood flow to the brain. It usually lasts a few minutes. Having a TIA can mean you are at risk for having a more serious stroke.</p>

<h3>What are the symptoms of ischemic stroke?</h3>
<p>Symptoms of stroke often happen suddenly. They include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Sudden numbness or weakness of the face, arm or leg (especially on one side of the body)</li>
<li>Sudden confusion, trouble speaking or understanding speech</li>
<li>Sudden trouble seeing in one or both eyes</li>
<li>Sudden trouble walking, dizziness, loss of balance or coordination</li>
<li>Sudden severe headache with no known cause</li>
</ul>

<p>If you think someone is having a stroke, the <strong>F.A.S.T.</strong> test can help you remember what to look for. Think "FAST" and look for:</p>
<ul> 
<li><strong>F</strong>ace drooping on one side when smiling.</li>
<li><strong>A</strong>rm weakness that occurs when the arms are raised, and one arm drifts downward.</li>
<li><strong>S</strong>peech is slurred or strange.</li>
<li><strong>T</strong>ime to call 911.</li>
</ul>

<h3>How is an ischemic stroke diagnosed?</h3>
<p>To make a diagnosis, your health care provider may do a physical exam, ask about your symptoms and medical history, and use <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/diagnosticimaging.html">imaging tests</a>. Other <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/hearthealthtests.html">heart tests</a> may be done to help find the cause of the stroke.</p>

<h3>How is an ischemic stroke treated?</h3>
<p>It is important to treat strokes as quickly as possible. The most common treatments include:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Medicines</strong> such as <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/bloodthinners.html">blood thinners</a> or thrombolytics (which break up and dissolve existing clots) may be used to prevent or treat ischemic stroke.</li> 
<li><strong>Surgery or stenting</strong> may be used to open narrowed arteries or remove plaque build-up from the carotid arteries, which supply blood to the brain.</li>
</ul>

<p>After treatment, <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/strokerehabilitation.html">rehabilitation</a> can help you regain lost skills and independence. Your provider may also order other medicines to manage your <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/bloodpressuremedicines.html">blood pressure</a>,<a href="https://medlineplus.gov/cholesterollevelswhatyouneedtoknow.html">cholesterol</a>, or other health problems such as <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/diabetescomplications.html">diabetes</a> that may increase your risk for stroke.</p>

<h3>Can an ischemic stroke be prevented?</h3>
<p>The best way to prevent stroke is by making <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/howtopreventheartdisease.html">heart-healthy lifestyle changes</a> to lower your risk. If lifestyle changes aren't enough, your provider may prescribe medicine to help manage your risk factors.</p>
