# Oral Cancer

<h3>What is oral cancer? </h3>
<p>Oral cancer is cancer of the mouth. It is a type of <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/headandneckcancer.html">head and neck cancer</a>. Most oral cancers are <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/genetics/condition/head-and-neck-squamous-cell-carcinoma/">squamous cell cancers</a>. They begin in the flat cells that cover the surfaces of your mouth, tongue, and lips. The cancer cells may spread into deeper tissue as the cancer grows.</p>

<p>Most oral cancers are related to tobacco use, heavy alcohol use, or an <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/hpv.html">HPV infection</a>.</p>

<h3>Who is more likely to develop oral cancer?</h3>
<p>Anyone can get oral cancer, but you are more likely to develop it if you:</p>
<ul>
<li>Use tobacco or drink lots of alcohol. Your risk of developing oral cancer is even higher if you do both.</li>
<li>Are male.</li>
<li>Are over ago 40.</li>
<li>Have HPV.</li>
<li>Have a history of head or neck cancer.</li>
<li>Get frequent <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/sunexposure.html">sun exposure</a> (for lip cancer).</li>
</ul>

<h3>What are the symptoms of oral cancer?</h3>
<p>The symptoms of oral cancer may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>A white or red patch in your mouth</li>
<li>A lip or mouth sore that won't heal</li>
<li>Bleeding, pain, or numbness in the lip or mouth</li>
<li>Loose teeth or dentures that no longer fit well</li>
<li>Problems or pain with <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/swallowingdisorders.html">swallowing</a></li>
<li>A lump in your neck</li>
<li>Ear pain</li>
<li>Trouble moving your mouth or jaw</li>
<li>Swelling of the jaw</li>
<li>A <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/sorethroat.html">sore throat</a> or feeling that something is caught in the throat</li>
</ul>

<p>If you have any of these symptoms for more than two weeks, see your health care provider or dentist. Oral cancer can spread quickly, so it's important to find it early.</p>

<h3>How is oral cancer diagnosed?</h3>
<p>To find out if you have oral cancer, your provider may use:</p>
<ul>
<li>A physical exam of the lips and mouth.</li>
<li>An <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/endoscopy.html">endoscopy</a>.</li>
<li>A <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/biopsy.html">biopsy</a> or other procedure to collect cells from the lip or oral cavity. The cells are viewed under a microscope to find out if they are abnormal.</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/diagnosticimaging.html">Imaging tests</a>.</li> 
</ul>

<h3>What are the treatments for oral cancer?</h3>
<p>The treatments for oral cancer include surgery, <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/radiationtherapy.html">radiation therapy</a>, or both. After surgery, some people also need <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/cancerchemotherapy.html">chemotherapy</a> to kill any cancer cells that are left.</p>

<h3>Can oral cancer be prevented?</h3>
<p>There are steps you can take to help prevent oral cancer:</p>
<ul>
<li>Not smoking</li>
<li>Limiting alcohol use or not drinking at all</li>
<li>Getting regular <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/dental-exam/">dental exams</a></li>
</ul>
