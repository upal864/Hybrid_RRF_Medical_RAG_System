# Fifth Disease

<h3>What is fifth disease?</h3>
<p>Fifth disease, also called erythema infectiosum, is a <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/viralinfections.html">viral infection</a> caused by parvovirus B19. This virus only infects people; it's not the same type of parvovirus that dogs and cats can get.</p> 

<p>Fifth disease mostly affects children. It often causes a bright red <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/rashes.html">rash</a> on their cheeks (sometimes called a "slapped cheek" rash).</p>

<p>In the United States, parvovirus B19 infections are more common in late winter, spring, and early summer. There are usually minor outbreaks of fifth disease about every 3 to 4 years.</p>

<h3>What causes fifth disease?</h3>
<p>Parvovirus B19, which causes fifth disease, can spread from person to person:</p>
<ul>
<li>Through respiratory droplets, such as saliva (spit), sputum, or nasal mucus. The droplets can spread when:
	<ul>
		<li>Someone who has the virus coughs and sneezes</li>
		<li>You touch an item that has the virus on it</li>
	</ul>
</li>
<li>Through blood or blood products</li>
<li>From a pregnant parent to the baby <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/infectionsandpregnancy.html">during pregnancy</a></li>
</ul>

<p>You are most contagious early in your infection, when you usually only have a fever or cold-like symptoms. You are not likely to be contagious after you get later symptoms such as a rash and joint pain.</p>

<h3>What are the symptoms of fifth disease?</h3>
<p>About 2 out of 10 people who get a B19 parvovirus infection will have no symptoms.</p>

<p>But most people will get the symptoms of fifth disease, which are usually mild and can include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/fever.html">Fever</a></li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/headache.html">Headache</a></li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/cough.html">Cough</a></li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/sorethroat.html">Sore throat</a></li>
<li>Rashes</li>
<li>Joint pain</li>
</ul>

<p>The "slapped cheek" rash is more common in children. They usually get the rash a few days after they first have other symptoms. The rash can then spread to the arms, legs, chest, back, and buttocks.</p>

<p>Adults who get fifth disease might also have joint pain and swelling (called polyarthropathy syndrome).</p>

<p>Sometimes the infection can cause serious health complications affecting the nerves, joints, or blood system. This is more likely to happen if you are pregnant, have a <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/blooddisorders.html">blood disorder</a> such as <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/anemia.html">anemia</a>, or a <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/immunesystemanddisorders.html">weakened immune system</a>.</p>

<p>Because of these risks to you and some possible risks to your baby, contact your health care provider if you are pregnant and:</p>
<ul>
<li>You show signs of fifth disease (or a "slapped cheek" rash)</li>
<li>You may have been exposed to someone who has the infection</li>
</ul>

<h3>How is fifth disease diagnosed?</h3>
<p>There is no routine lab test to diagnose fifth disease. Providers can often diagnose fifth disease just by seeing the "slapped cheek" rash and rashes on the body.</p> 

<p>There is testing to show whether or not you have protection (immunity) against the B19 parvovirus or if you have had a recent infection. This test is usually not needed, but your provider may order the test if you are pregnant or at higher risk for complications of the disease.</p>

<h3>What are the treatments for fifth disease?</h3>
<p>There is no specific treatment for fifth disease. It is usually mild and goes away on its own.</p> 

<p>To feel better, get plenty of rest. Taking acetaminophen can help with the fever and pain. Do not give aspirin to children, because it may cause <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/reyesyndrome.html">Reye syndrome</a>. This is a rare, serious illness that can affect the brain and liver.</p>

<h3>Can fifth disease be prevented?</h3>
<p>There is no vaccine to prevent fifth disease. But you can help protect yourself and others by:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/germsandhygiene.html">Washing your hands</a> often with soap and water</li>
<li>Covering your mouth and nose when you cough or sneeze</li>
<li>Avoiding touching your eyes, nose, and mouth with unwashed hands</li> 
<li>Avoiding close contact with people who are sick</li>
<li>Staying home when you are sick</li>
</ul>

<p>Most people become immune to the virus after having it once.</p>
