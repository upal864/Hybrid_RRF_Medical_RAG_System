# Diverticulosis and Diverticulitis

<h3>What is diverticulosis?</h3>
<p>Diverticula are small pouches, or sacs, that bulge outward through weak spots in your colon. They mostly form in the lower part of the colon. Diverticulosis is a condition in which you have these pouches. Most people who have diverticulosis do not have symptoms or problems. But sometimes the pouches can cause symptoms or become inflamed.</p>

<h3>What is diverticulitis?</h3>
<p>Diverticulitis is the name for the condition you have when one or more of the pouches get inflamed. Diverticulitis may come on suddenly. It can sometimes cause serious health problems.</p>

<h3>What is diverticular disease?</h3>
<p>Diverticular disease is a condition that happens when the pouches cause:</p>
<ul>
<li>Chronic (long-term) symptoms</li>
<li>Diverticular bleeding</li>
<li>Diverticulitis or diverticulitis complications</li>
</ul>

<h3>What causes diverticulosis and diverticulitis?</h3>
<p>Researchers aren't sure what causes diverticulosis and diverticulitis. They think certain factors may play a role in causing or increasing the risk for these conditions, including:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Your genetics.</strong> Certain genes may make some people more likely to develop the conditions.</li>
<li><strong>Lifestyle factors</strong> such as:
  <ul>
  <li>Diets low in <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/dietaryfiber.html">fiber</a> and high in red meat</li>
  <li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/healthrisksofaninactivelifestyle.html">Lack of physical activity</a></li>
  <li>Taking certain medicines, such as <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/painrelievers.html">nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs</a> (NSAIDs) and <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/steroids.html">steroids</a></li>
  <li>Having <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/obesity.html">obesity</a></li>
  <li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/smoking.html">Smoking</a></li>
  </ul>
</li>
</ul>

<p>Researchers are also looking at other possible factors that may play a role in these conditions. Those factors include bacteria or stool (poop) getting caught in a pouch in your colon and changes in the microbiome in the intestines. Your microbiome is made up of the bacteria and other organisms in your intestines.</p>

<h3>Who is more likely to develop diverticulosis and diverticulitis?</h3>
<p>Diverticulosis is common, especially as people age. More than one-third of U.S. adults between the ages of 50 and 59 have diverticulosis. More than two-thirds who are over age 80 have it. Most of those people will not have symptoms or problems. But some of them will develop diverticulitis.</p>

<h3>What are the symptoms of diverticulosis and diverticulitis?</h3>
<p>Diverticulosis usually doesn't cause symptoms. But some people can have chronic symptoms such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Bloating</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/constipation.html">Constipation</a> or <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/diarrhea.html">diarrhea</a></li>
<li>Cramping or <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/abdominalpain.html">pain</a>in the lower abdomen (belly)</li>
</ul>

<p>Diverticulitis may cause acute symptoms such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Abdominal pain, most often in the lower left side of your abdomen</li>
<li>Constipation or diarrhea</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/fever.html">Fevers</a> and chills</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/nauseaandvomiting.html">Nausea or vomiting</a></li>
</ul>

<p>The pain caused by diverticulitis is usually severe and comes on suddenly. Less often, the pain may be mild and worsen over several days.</p>

<h3>What other problems can diverticulosis and diverticulitis cause?</h3>
<p>Some people with diverticulosis and diverticulitis may develop serious health problems (complications). Diverticular bleeding happens when a small blood vessel within the wall of a pouch bursts. The bleeding may be severe and sometimes even life-threatening.</p>

<p>People with diverticulitis can also develop serious problems such as:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/abscess.html">Abscess</a>, a painful, swollen, pus-filled area caused by infection</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/fistulas.html">Fistula</a>, an abnormal opening or passage between the colon and another part of the body, such as the bladder or vagina</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/intestinalobstruction.html">Intestinal obstruction</a>, a partial or total blockage that keeps food, fluids, air, or stool from moving through your intestines</li><li>Perforation, or a hole, in your colon</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/peritonealdisorders.html">Peritonitis</a>, an infection of the lining of the abdominal cavity</li>
</ul>

<h3>How are diverticulosis and diverticulitis diagnosed?</h3>
<p>Diverticulosis may be found when your health care provider is doing tests for another reason. Diverticulitis is usually found when you are having an acute attack.</p>

<p>To make a diagnosis, your provider will review your medical history, do a physical exam, and order tests. The tests may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Blood tests</li>
<li>Stool tests</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/diagnosticimaging.html">Imaging tests</a> such as <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/ctscans.html">CT scan</a>, <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/sonogram/">ultrasound</a>, or <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/mriscans.html">MRI</a></li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/colonoscopy.html">Colonoscopy</a></li>
</ul>

<h3>What are the treatments for diverticulosis and diverticulitis?</h3>
<p>If your diverticulosis is causing chronic symptoms, your provider may recommend:</p>
<ul>
<li>High-fiber foods or fiber supplements</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/antibiotics.html">Antibiotics</a></li>
<li>Medicines to reduce inflammation</li>
<li>Probiotics</li>
</ul>

<p>If you have diverticulitis without complications, your provider may recommend treatment at home. However, you probably need treatment in the hospital if you have severe diverticulitis, diverticulitis with complications, or a high risk for complications. </p>

<p>Treatments for diverticulitis may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Antibiotics, except for very mild cases.</li>
<li>A clear liquid diet for a short time to rest the colon. Your provider may suggest slowly adding solid foods to your diet as your symptoms improve.</li>
<li>Medicines for pain. This is usually acetaminophen instead of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs). NSAIDs may increase the chance of diverticulitis complications.</li>
<li>Antispasmodic medicines to relieve spasms.</li>
</ul>

<p>If your diverticulitis doesn't improve with treatment or if it causes complications, you may need surgery to remove part of your colon.</p>

<h3>Can diverticulitis be prevented?</h3>
<p>Your provider may recommend lifestyle changes to prevent diverticulitis:</p>
<ul>
<li>Eating a diet high in fiber and low in red meat</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/howmuchexercisedoineed.html">Being physically active on a regular basis</a></li>
<li>Not smoking (and <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/quittingsmoking.html">quitting smoking</a> if you are a smoker)</li>
<li>Reaching and maintaining a <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/weightcontrol.html">healthy weigh</a>t</li>
</ul>
