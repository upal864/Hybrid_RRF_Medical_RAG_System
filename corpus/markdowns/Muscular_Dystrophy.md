# Muscular Dystrophy

<h3>What is muscular dystrophy (MD)?</h3>
<p>Muscular dystrophy (MD) is a group of more than 30 <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/geneticdisorders.html">genetic diseases</a>. They cause weakness of the muscles. Over time, the weakness gets worse and can cause trouble <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/walkingproblems.html">walking</a> and doing daily activities. Some types of MD can also affect other organs.</p>

<h3>What are the types of muscular dystrophy (MD)?</h3>
<p>There are many different types of MD. Some of the more common types include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Duchenne muscular dystrophy, which is the most common childhood form. It is severe and affects boys more often than girls. The symptoms usually start between ages 3 and 6.</li>
<li>Becker muscular dystrophy, which is similar to Duchenne but is less severe and gets worse more slowly. It often starts in the teenage years.</li>
<li>Congenital muscular dystrophies, which are present at birth or before age 2. They can be mild or severe.</li>
<li>Facioscapulohumeral muscular dystrophy, which often starts in the teenage years. At first, it affects the muscles of the face, shoulders, and upper arms.</li>
</ul>

<p>Each of the types of MD can be different in many ways, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Who is more likely to get them</li>
<li>Which muscles they affect</li>
<li>When they appear, such as in infancy, childhood, middle age, or later</li>
<li>What the symptoms are</li>
<li>How serious the symptoms are</li>
<li>How quickly they get worse</li>
<li>Whether they run in families</li>
<li>Whether they affect other organs</li>
</ul>

<p>Even within the same type of MD, people can have different symptoms.</p>

<h3>What causes muscular dystrophy (MD)?</h3>
<p>MD is genetic, meaning that it caused by a change in one or more genes. Gene changes are also called gene variants or mutations. The gene changes in MD affect proteins that strengthen and protect muscles.</p> 

<p>There are different gene changes that cause each type of MD. And sometimes people who have the same type of MD can  have different gene changes.</p>

<p>Muscular dystrophy can run in families, or you can be the first in your family to have a muscular dystrophy.</p>

<h3>How is muscular dystrophy (MD) diagnosed?</h3>
<p>To find out if you or your child has MD, your health care provider may use:</p>
<ul>
<li>A medical and family history</li>
<li>A physical exam</li>
<li>Blood and urine tests, including <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/genetictesting.html">genetic tests</a> and tests for certain enzymes that may be released by damaged muscles</li>
<li>Muscle <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/biopsy.html">biopsies</a></li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/electromyography-emg-and-nerve-conduction-studies/">Electromyography and nerve conduction studies</a> to find out if muscles are responding the right way to nerve signals</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/hearthealthtests.html">Heart testing</a>, such as an <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/electrocardiogram/">electrocardiogram</a> (EKG), since some types of MD can cause <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/heartdiseases.html">heart problems</a></li>
<li>Exercise tests to measure muscle strength and breathing and detect any increased rates of certain chemicals following exercise</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/diagnosticimaging.html">Imaging tests</a> such as an <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/mriscans.html">MRI</a> to look at muscle quality and bulk and measure fatty replacement of muscle tissue</li>
</ul>

<h3>What are the treatments for muscular dystrophy (MD)?</h3>
<p>There is no cure for muscular dystrophy. Treatment can help with the symptoms and prevent complications. It usually includes a combination of therapies, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Physical therapy to help keep muscles flexible and strong</li>
<li>Occupational therapy to relearn lost motor skills and learn ways to work around weakened muscles</li>
<li>Respiratory care, such as breathing exercises, <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/oxygentherapy.html">oxygen therapy</a>, and ventilators</li>
<li>Speech therapy to help with <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/speechandcommunicationdisorders.html">speech</a> and <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/swallowingdisorders.html">swallowing</a> problems</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/assistivedevices.html">Assistive devices</a>, such as wheelchairs, splints and braces, and walkers</li>
<li>Medicines to help delay damage to muscles or minimize the symptoms of MD</li>
<li>Surgery to treat some of the conditions associated with MD, such as heart problems, <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/scoliosis.html">scoliosis</a>, and <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/cataract.html">cataracts</a></li>
</ul>
