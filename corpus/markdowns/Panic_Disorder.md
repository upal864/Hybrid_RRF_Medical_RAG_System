# Panic Disorder

<h3>What is panic disorder?</h3> 
<p>Panic disorder is a type of <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/anxiety.html">anxiety disorder</a>.  It causes repeated panic attacks, which are sudden periods of intense fear, discomfort, or a sense of losing control. These attacks happen even though there is no real danger. They often cause physical symptoms. For example, you may have a rapid or pounding heartbeat and feel like you are having a <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/heartattack.html">heart attack</a>.</p>

<p>If you have panic attacks, it doesn't mean you will develop a panic disorder. Many people only have one or two panic attacks in their lifetime and get better without treatment.</p> 

<p>But some of the people who have panic attacks do develop panic disorder. They have repeated panic attacks. The attacks can happen as often as several times a day or as rarely as a few times a year. People with panic disorder often worry about having another attack. It may cause them to avoid places and situations where they had panic attacks in the past.</p> 

<p>Panic disorder is not life-threatening, but it can be upsetting and affect your quality of life. And if it is not treated, it can sometimes lead to other health conditions, including <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/depression.html">depression</a> and substance use disorders.</p>

<h3>What causes panic disorder?</h3>
<p>The cause of panic disorder is unknown. Researchers think that certain factors may play a role:</p>
<ul> 
<li>Genetics - panic disorder sometimes runs in families. But no one knows for sure why some family members have it while others don't.</li>
<li>Brain biology and chemistry.</li>
<li>Your environment.</li>
<li>Major <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/stress.html">stress</a>.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Who is more likely to develop panic disorder?</h3> 
<p>Panic disorder is more common in women than men. It often starts in the late teens or early adulthood. Sometimes it starts when a person is under a lot of stress. People who have had trauma, especially in childhood, are more likely to develop panic disorder.</p>

<h3>What are the symptoms of panic disorder?</h3>
<p>People with panic disorder may have:</p>
<ul> 
<li>Sudden and repeated panic attacks of overwhelming anxiety and fear</li>
<li>A feeling of being out of control or a fear of death during a panic attack</li>
<li>An intense worry about another panic attack</li>
<li>A fear or avoidance of places and situations where they had panic attacks in the past</li>
<li>Physical symptoms during a panic attack, such as:
	<ul>
		<li>Pounding or racing heart</li>
		<li>Sweating or chills</li>
		<li>Trembling or shaking</li>
		<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/breathingproblems.html">Trouble breathing</a></li>
		<li>The feeling that they are choking</li>
		<li>Weakness or <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/dizzinessandvertigo.html">dizziness</a></li>
		<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/chestpain.html">Chest pain</a></li>
		<li>Stomach pain or <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/nauseaandvomiting.html">nausea</a></li>
	</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<p>Panic attacks can happen anytime, without warning. They can last anywhere from a few minutes to over an hour.</p>

<h3>How is panic disorder diagnosed?</h3>
<p>To find out if you have panic disorder, your health care provider:</p>
<ul>
<li>Will ask about your medical history and symptoms</li>
<li>May check if an unrelated physical problem is causing your symptoms, for example with:
	<ul>
		<li>A physical exam</li>
		<li>Blood tests to check for <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/thyroiddiseases.html">thyroid problems</a> and other possible conditions</li>
		<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/hearthealthtests.html">Heart health tests</a></li>
	</ul>
</li>
<li>May do a<a href="https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/panic-disorder-test/"> panic disorder test</a></li> 
<li>May refer you to a mental health provider for the panic disorder test or other types of psychological evaluations</li> 
</ul>

<h3>What are the treatments for panic disorder?</h3>
<p>Treatment for panic disorder usually includes one or more of the following:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Talk therapy</strong>, also called psychotherapy or counseling, which can help you understand your feelings.
	<ul>
		<li>It may include <strong>cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT),</strong> a type of talk therapy that helps you change your negative thoughts and how you react to things that cause you to feel anxiety.</li>
	</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Medicines</strong>, including:
	<ul>
		<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/antidepressants.html">Antidepressants</a>, such as selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) and serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs)</li> 
		<li>Anti-anxiety medicines</li> 
	</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<p>Your provider may also suggest that you follow a healthy lifestyle, which may help with panic disorder. It may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Avoiding alcohol</li>
<li>Reducing <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/caffeine.html">caffeine</a></li>
<li>Eating regular meals</li> 
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/healthysleep.html">Getting enough sleep</a> </li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/howmuchexercisedoineed.html">Getting regular exercise</a></li>
</ul>

<p>Joining a support group may also be helpful. Support groups can make you feel like you are not alone, and you may learn some new tips on how to cope.</p>
