# CT Scans

<h3>What is a CT scan?</h3>
<p>Computed tomography (CT) is a type of medical <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/diagnosticimaging.html">imaging</a> test that uses special <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/xrays.html">x-ray</a> equipment to make detailed pictures of the inside of your body.</p>

<p>A CT scan produces cross-sectional pictures, which are like slices of the body. These images show the size and structure of organs, bones, and other tissues. CT images can also be combined to create 3-dimensional (3D) views.</p>

<p>Health care providers use CT scans to diagnose, treat, and monitor many health conditions.</p>

<h3>Who needs a CT scan?</h3>
<p>Your provider may suggest a CT scan for many reasons, including:</p>
<ul>

<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/fractures.html">Fractures</a> (broken bones)</li>
<li>Cancer</li>
<li>Tumors or masses</li>
<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/bloodclots.html">Blood clots</a></li>
<li>Signs of <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/heartdiseases.html">heart disease</a></li>
<li>Spinal conditions</li>
<li>Lung disease</li>

</ul>

<p>CT scans may also be used to find the best place to perform a <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/biopsy.html">biopsy</a> or help guide certain treatments. They may also be used to check for internal bleeding or injury after trauma or to evaluate the brain for certain conditions.</p>

<h3>How do you prepare for a CT scan?</h3>
<p>You may be asked not to eat or drink for a few hours before the scan. Ask your provider if there are any special instructions to follow.</p>

<p>For some CT scans, you may receive a contrast material. Contrast helps certain parts of the body show up more clearly on the images. Depending on what area is being examined, contrast material might be given as:</p>
<ul>
<li>A liquid to swallow</li>
<li>A shot (injection)</li>
<li>An enema (placed into the rectum)</li>
</ul>
<p>CT scans use ionizing <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/radiationexposure.html">radiation</a> at levels higher than a standard x-ray, but the amount is kept as low as possible. Always tell your provider if you are pregnant or think you may be pregnant.</p>

<h3>How is a CT scan done?</h3>
<p>You may be asked to change clothes and remove jewelry or other items that could interfere with the scan.</p> 

<p>During the scan, you lie still on a table. The table slowly passes through the center of a large donut-shaped machine. The x-ray tube rotates around your body to create images.</p>

<p>You may hear whirring sounds during the scan. You may be asked to hold your breath for short periods. The test is painless.</p>

<p>The scan usually takes a few minutes, but some may last up to 30 minutes, depending on the area being scanned.</p>

<h3>What should you expect after a CT scan?</h3>
<p>Most people can return to normal activities right away. If you were given a contrast material, drinking fluids can help your body clear it.</p>

<p>A specialist called a radiologist reviews the images and shares the results with your provider.</p>
