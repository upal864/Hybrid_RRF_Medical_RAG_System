# Sexual Assault

<h3>What is sexual assault?</h3>
<p>Sexual assault is any kind of sexual activity, contact, or experience that happens <strong>without</strong> your consent. That means the sexual activity happens even though you don't agree to it.</p>
  
<p>Sexual assault may happen with:</p>
<ul>
<li>Physical force or threats of force</li>
<li>Sexual coercion (being pressured, tricked, threatened, or forced in a nonphysical way to make you think you owe sex to someone)</li> 
<li>Alcohol or drugs, including <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/clubdrugs.html">date rape drugs</a></li>
</ul>

<p>Sexual assault may also be called sexual violence or sexual abuse. It affects millions of people each year. Anyone can be a victim of sexual assault. Many victims first experience <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/childsexualabuse.html">sexual assault during childhood</a>. Most victims are girls and women. But many boys and men are also victims of sexual assault.</p> 
<p>If you've been sexually assaulted, it's never your fault. It's something that happens <em>to</em> you.</p>

<h3>What does sexual assault include?</h3>
<p>Sexual assault is any <strong>unwanted</strong> sexual activity, including:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sexual activity with physical contact</strong>, which is everything from sexual touching or kissing to rape or attempted rape</li>  
<li><strong>Sexual activity without physical contact</strong>, which includes things that may happen in person, online, or through texts, such as:
	<ul>
		<li>"Flashing" or exhibitionism (when someone shows you their genitals without asking you for permission)</li>
		<li>Being forced to look at sexual images or pose for sexual photos</li>
		<li>Being sent unwanted texts with sexual photos or messages</li>
		<li>Voyeurism (when someone watches private sexual acts without permission)</li>
		<li>Getting sexual threats or being sexually harassed (unwelcome sexual comments, requests for sexual favors, or other sexual behavior that's ongoing)</li>
	</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<p>Any sexual activity that happens without your consent is sexual assault.</p>

<h3>What does "consent" mean?</h3>
<p>Giving your consent means that you clearly and freely say "yes" to sexual activity. Your consent means that you:</p>
<ul>
<li>Know and understand what's going on</li>
<li>Can say what you want to do and don't want to do</li>
<li>Are not underage</li>
<li>Are not being pressured or tricked to do things you don't want to do</li>
</ul>

<p>Giving consent is <strong>not</strong>:</p>
<ul> 
<li><strong>Silence.</strong> Not saying "no" doesn't mean you're saying "yes."</li> 
<li><strong>Having consented in the past.</strong> Having said "yes" to sex with someone in the past doesn't mean that you've agreed to all sexual activity in the future. That's true when you're dating or in a lasting relationship.</li> 
<li><strong>Being passive</strong> and not fighting off an attacker.</li> 
</ul>

<p>You cannot give consent if you are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Drugged, drunk, passed out, or asleep.</li>
<li>Mentally unable to consent due to illness or disability.</li>
<li>Underage (States have different laws about how old you must be to give legal consent to sexual activity.).</li>
<li>Sexually coerced. This means that someone is pressuring you to take part in sexual activity. Agreeing to sex under pressure is <strong>not</strong> giving consent. The pressure may include:
	<ul>
		<li>Abusing power over you, for example, a boss threatening your job.</li> 
		<li>Making threats to harm people you care about.</li>
		<li>Making false promises to reward you for sex.</li>
		<li>Threatening to end a relationship or spread rumors about you.</li>
		<li>Wearing you down by constantly asking for sex or making you feel guilty or obligated.</li> 
	</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<h3>Who commits sexual assault?</h3>
<p>Both men and women commit sexual assault. A person who commits sexual assault may be a stranger, but more often it's someone you know, for example:</p>
<ul>
<li>A friend or acquaintance</li>
<li>Someone you work with</li>
<li>A neighbor</li>
<li>A family member</li>
<li>A current or past romantic partner, including a spouse</li>
</ul>

<h3>What are the possible health effects from sexual assault?</h3> 
<p>Sexual assault may have many health effects. Many of them can affect your lifelong health and well-being. The effects may include:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Physical effects</strong> such as:
	<ul>
		<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/bruises.html">Bruises</a> or genital injuries</li>
		<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/sexuallytransmittedinfections.html">Sexually transmitted infections</a> (STIs)</li>
		<li>Pregnancy</li>
		<li>Ongoing problems with:
			<ul>
				<li>Female reproductive health</li>
				<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/digestivediseases.html">Digestive disorders</a></li>
				<li>Your <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/heartdiseases.html">heart</a></li>
				<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/sexualhealth.html">Sexual health</a></li>
			</ul>
		</li>
	</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Psychological effects</strong> such as:
	<ul>
		<li><a href="https://medlineplus.gov/depression.html">Depression</a> or <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/anxiety.html">anxiety</a></li>
		<li>Thoughts about <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/suicide.html">suicide</a></li>
		<li><a href="posttraumaticstressdisorder.html" tid="367">Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)</a></li>
	</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<p>Sexual assault victims are more likely to <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/smoking.html">smoke</a>, have  an <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/alcoholusedisorderaud.html">alcohol use disorder</a> (AUD), <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/druguseandaddiction.html">use illegal drugs</a>, and have risky sexual behavior. Girls who experience <a href="https://medlineplus.gov/intimatepartnerviolence.html">sexual violence</a> are more likely to become victims of intimate partner violence in adulthood.</p>

<h3>What can I do if I'm a victim of sexual assault?</h3>
<p><strong>If you are in danger or need medical care, call 911.</strong> If you can, get away from the person who assaulted you and get to a safe place as fast as you can.</p> 

<p>You can call the National Sexual Assault Hotline at 800-656-HOPE (4673) to connect with a sexual assault service provider in your area who can direct you to local resources.</p>

<p>Getting support after a sexual assault may help you cope better and reduce the long-term effects. Victim services like rape crisis centers may offer a safe, healing place where you can find support.</p>

<p>Studies show that treatments such as Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (TF-CBT) can help victims cope with depression, anxiety, and post-traumatic stress disorder.</p>

<h3>Can sexual assault be prevented?</h3>
<p>Sexual assault is never the victim's fault. No one who has been assaulted should be blamed for something that they did or did not do.</p> 

<p>You can take some steps to try to be safer around others:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Go to parties with friends.</strong> Look out for each other. If a friend seems drunk or drugged, get them to a safe place. Have a plan for how you'll get home, too.</li>
<li><strong>Keep control of your drink.</strong> Someone could add alcohol or put a drug in it without you knowing.</li>
<li><strong>Meet people for the first time in a public place.</strong></li>
<li><strong>Listen to your "gut feelings."</strong> If you feel uncomfortable around someone, leave.</li>
<li><strong>Pay attention to your surroundings.</strong> When walking alone, stay in busy well-lit areas. And don't wear headphones; you want to be able to hear what's happening around you.</li>
</ul>
